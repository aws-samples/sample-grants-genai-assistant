

# DoD Cyber Service Academy (CSA) Grant Proposal — Brainstorming & Notes Document

---

## OVERVIEW & KEY OBSERVATIONS

**Grant Program:** DoD Cyber Service Academy (CSA) — authorized by 10 U.S.C. Chapter 112, Section 2200

**Core Purpose:** Recruitment of new cyber talent + retention of current DoD cyber workforce professionals. Enhance the national pipeline for cyber personnel development.

**What They're Funding:**
- Full-time, institution-based, grant-funded **scholarship program** in cyber-related disciplines for AY 2024-2025
- Optional "modest collateral support" for **institutional capacity building**: faculty development, lab improvements, curriculum development

**Eligibility Requirements:**
- Must be regionally/nationally accredited U.S. institution of higher education
- Must hold **NCAE-C designation** (Cyber Defense, Research, and/or Cyber Operations)
- Must be in good standing with NCAE-C Program Office — no delinquent documentation
- Can collaborate with other accredited institutions
- **Encouraged** to include accredited post-secondary **minority institutions** (this is a scoring/priority signal — we should lean into this)

**Key Realization:** This is NOT a traditional research BAA. This is a **scholarship program + capacity building grant**. The three prompt templates (Technical Approach, Execution Plan, Military Relevance) are generic DoD proposal templates that need to be **adapted** to fit what is essentially an education/workforce development program, not a research project. The "technical approach" here is really about **program design** — how you recruit, select, educate, mentor, and transition scholars into the DoD cyber workforce. The "military relevance" is about **workforce pipeline impact**, not weapon system development.

### 1.2 Current State of the Art and Gaps → Current Cyber Workforce Landscape & Gaps

**Notes:** This section should paint the picture of the cyber workforce crisis that justifies this program.

**Key Points to Develop:**

- **National cyber workforce shortage:** The (ISC)² Cybersecurity Workforce Study consistently reports hundreds of thousands of unfilled cybersecurity positions in the U.S. The DoD competes with private sector for the same talent pool, often at a salary disadvantage.
- **DoD-specific workforce challenges:**
  - CYBERCOM and service cyber components have documented manning shortfalls
  - DoD Cyber Workforce Strategy (publicly available document) identifies recruitment and retention as critical challenges
  - Security clearance timelines create additional friction in hiring pipeline
  - Competition with private sector salaries (Google, Amazon, etc. offering $150K+ for entry-level cyber roles)
- **Diversity gap:** Cybersecurity workforce remains overwhelmingly non-diverse. Women represent roughly 24% of the global cybersecurity workforce; racial/ethnic minorities are similarly underrepresented. DoD needs diverse perspectives for mission effectiveness.
- **Education pipeline gaps:**
  - Many cyber programs are theoretical and lack hands-on, operationally relevant training
  - Students graduate without exposure to DoD-specific tools, frameworks, and operational environments
  - Limited integration between academic programs and DoD hiring/internship pipelines
  - Faculty at many institutions lack current operational cyber experience
  - Lab environments often don't replicate real-world DoD network architectures or threat scenarios

## SECTION 3: MILITARY RELEVANCE AND IMPACT

### 3.1 Relevance to DoD Mission

**Key framing:** This is a **workforce development** program, so military relevance is about **people**, not technology.

**Strategic Documents to Reference (all publicly available):**
- **National Defense Strategy** — consistently identifies cyber as a critical domain; workforce is an enabler
- **DoD Cyber Strategy** — explicitly calls for growing and sustaining the cyber workforce
- **DoD Cyber Workforce Strategy** — the most directly relevant document; outlines pillars of recruit, develop, retain, and manage cyber talent
- **National Cybersecurity Strategy (2023)** — calls for investing in cyber workforce development
- **CYBERCOM's stated workforce needs** — persistent shortfalls in cyber mission force manning
- **Executive Order on Improving the Nation's Cybersecurity (EO 14028)** — workforce provisions
- **10 U.S.C. Chapter 112** — the very statute authorizing this program; demonstrates Congressional recognition of the need

**Key Arguments:**
- The DoD cannot execute its cyber mission without qualified people. Every unfilled cyber billet is a capability gap.
- The cyber threat landscape is expanding (nation-state actors, ransomware, supply chain attacks) — the workforce must grow to match.
- DoD competes with private sector for a limited talent pool — scholarship programs with service obligations are a proven recruitment mechanism (analogous to ROTC for military officers).
- Diversity in the cyber workforce isn't just an equity issue — it's a national security issue. Diverse teams produce better analytical outcomes, and the DoD needs to draw from the entire national talent pool.
- NCAE-C institutions are the DoD's designated centers of excellence for cyber education — investing in them is investing in the foundation of the pipeline.

### 3.2 Operational Scenarios and Military Applications → Workforce Impact Scenarios

**Reframe:** Instead of operational scenarios for a weapon system, describe workforce scenarios.

**Scenario 1: Cyber Mission Force Manning**
- Context: CYBERCOM's Cyber Mission Force (CMF) teams require trained cyber operators and analysts. Teams operate below full strength due to recruitment and retention challenges.
- How this program helps: Graduates enter DoD as trained cyber professionals, filling billets on CMF teams or in supporting roles. Their NCAE-C education provides foundational knowledge in network defense, threat analysis, and cyber operations.
- Improvement over current state: Reduces time-to-proficiency for new hires; scholars arrive with hands-on lab experience and potentially relevant certifications.

**Scenario 2: DoD Network Defense**
- Context: DoD operates one of the largest and most targeted network infrastructures in the world. DISA, service cyber components, and agency CISOs need qualified cybersecurity professionals for network defense, incident response, and security engineering.
- How this program helps: Scholars trained in network defense, security architecture, incident response, and risk management fill critical civilian cybersecurity positions across DoD.
- Improvement: Reduces reliance on expensive contractors for core cybersecurity functions; builds organic DoD capability.

**Scenario 3: Emerging Technology Security**
- Context: DoD is rapidly adopting cloud computing, AI/ML, IoT, and 5G — each introducing new attack surfaces. The workforce must understand security implications of these technologies.
- How this program helps: Curriculum development component (capacity building) ensures scholars are trained on emerging technology security, not just legacy systems. Faculty development keeps instructors current.
- Improvement: Graduates are prepared for the cyber challenges of tomorrow, not just today.

### 3.3 Warfighter Benefits

**Frame as workforce/organizational benefits:**

- **Improved cyber defense posture:** More qualified defenders = better protected networks = more reliable C2, logistics, and intelligence systems for warfighters
- **Reduced risk to operations:** Cyber attacks on DoD systems can have kinetic consequences (e.g., disrupting logistics, degrading weapons systems). A stronger cyber workforce reduces this risk.
- **Enhanced operational effectiveness:** Cyber-enabled operations (offensive and defensive) require skilled operators. This program feeds that pipeline.
- **Cost efficiency:** Growing organic DoD cyber talent is more cost-effective long-term than relying on contractor support for core functions
- **Improved readiness:** Fully manned cyber units are ready units. Reducing vacancy rates directly improves readiness metrics.
- **Diversity of thought:** Scholars from diverse backgrounds (especially from minority institution partnerships) bring different perspectives to problem-solving — critical in the adversarial, creative domain of cybersecurity

### 3.4 Strategic Value and Impact

- **Maintaining technological superiority:** The U.S. military's technological edge depends on secure systems. Secure systems depend on qualified people. This program is a foundational investment in that chain.
- **Great power competition:** Near-peer adversaries (China, Russia) are investing heavily in cyber capabilities and cyber workforce. The U.S. must match or exceed this investment in human capital.
- **Whole-of-nation approach:** The CSA program, by investing in NCAE-C institutions, strengthens the entire national cyber education ecosystem — benefits flow to DoD, other government agencies, and the private sector.
- **Long-term pipeline sustainability:** Capacity building investments (faculty, labs, curriculum) create lasting institutional capability that produces cyber professionals for decades, not just one grant cycle.
- **Interagency benefit:** Scholars who complete their DoD service obligation and later move to DHS, FBI, IC, or private sector still contribute to national cybersecurity — the investment has compounding returns.

### 3.5 Transition Potential and Pathway

**For this program, "transition" = scholars transitioning into the DoD workforce**

- **Readiness:** Scholars graduate with relevant degrees, hands-on experience, and (ideally) security clearances in process — they are ready to contribute from day one.
- **Integration points:**
  - Cyber Excepted Service (CES) hiring authorities — streamlined hiring for cyber positions
  - DoD Civilian cyber positions across all services and agencies
  - Military commissioning or enlistment with cyber MOS/rating/AFSC
  - DoD contractor positions (as a secondary pathway)
  - NSA, DIA, and other IC agencies with cyber missions
- **Potential DoD stakeholders interested in graduates:**
  - U.S. Cyber Command (CYBERCOM)
  - National Security Agency (NSA)
  - Defense Information Systems Agency (DISA)
  - Service cyber components (Army Cyber, Fleet Cyber Command, 16th Air Force)
  - Service CIOs and CISOs
  - Defense agencies with cyber needs (DLA, DFAS, etc.)
- **Barriers to adoption and mitigation:**
  - Security clearance processing time → initiate early in program
  - Geographic constraints → remote work options increasingly available in DoD cyber
  - Salary competition with private sector → emphasize mission, benefits, job security, public service loan forgiveness
  - Hiring process complexity → partner with DoD HR to streamline; leverage CES authorities
