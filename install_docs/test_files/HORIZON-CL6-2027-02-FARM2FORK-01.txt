The call is expected to prioritise:

    Systemic approaches linking water management, nutrient circularity, and digital innovation across diverse pedoclimatic zones.

2.1 Responding to Bondalti's Five Capability Areas

We structure our complementary contributions against the five areas you have outlined:

Area 1 — Digital water & nutrient monitoring systems: Your strength in designing monitoring architectures for irrigation water quality and nutrient availability is a critical hardware-software backbone. We complement this with agronomic interpretation layers — translating sensor outputs into crop-specific nutrient demand models, phenological stage adjustments, and soil-water balance calculations. Our experience with heterogeneous farm typologies (small-scale, organic, mixed farming) ensures that the monitoring system design accounts for the diversity of end-user contexts the call expects. We also bring expertise in edge computing for low-connectivity environments, which aligns with your acknowledgment of connectivity limitations in field deployments.

Area 2 — Reclaimed water and circular nutrient pathways: This is arguably one of the most innovative and policy-relevant dimensions of the proposal. Bondalti's capacity to engineer fit-for-purpose reuse configurations is essential, and we strengthen this with field-level agronomic trials assessing the long-term effects of reclaimed water irrigation on soil health, crop quality, microbial ecology, and nutrient accumulation. We also contribute regulatory navigation support, having worked closely with national authorities on the implementation of Regulation (EU) 2020/741 on minimum requirements for water reuse. This dual lens — engineering feasibility paired with agronomic evidence and regulatory compliance — significantly de-risks the circular water narrative for evaluators.

Area 3 — AI-enabled decision support and digital twins: We bring domain-specific modelling capabilities in crop-water-nutrient dynamics (e.g., DSSAT, AquaCrop, APSIM frameworks) that can feed into or interoperate with Bondalti's AI-DSS architecture. Our researchers have published extensively on the calibration and validation of such models under deficit irrigation and variable nutrient regimes across Mediterranean and semi-arid contexts. For the digital twin component, we offer spatially explicit farm-level datasets (soil maps, microclimate records, yield histories) from our long-term experimental stations, which are invaluable for training and validating twin models.

Area 4 — High-quality datasets for AI training (FAIR-by-design): We strongly endorse your commitment to FAIR-by-design data principles and note this as an increasingly weighted evaluation criterion. Our contribution includes existing curated datasets from multi-year field experiments (soil moisture, nutrient leaching, crop physiological responses, water quality parameters) that are already partially harmonised under previous EU project protocols. We propose jointly developing a data governance framework early in the project, defining collection standards, metadata schemas (aligned with INSPIRE and ISO 28258 for soil data), quality assurance/quality control (QA/QC) pipelines, and embargo/open-access timelines. We have a dedicated data steward with experience in Zenodo, EOSC, and domain-specific repositories.

Area 5 — Translation to practice: affordability, trust, accessibility, and SSH: This is where many technically strong proposals fail — by treating SSH integration as a box-ticking exercise. We propose a genuine co-design methodology rooted in participatory action research, involving farmers and advisors from proposal stage through to validation. Our team includes social scientists specialising in technology adoption in agriculture, with published work on trust dynamics in digital farming tools, the role of peer networks in diffusion, and barriers faced by aging farmer populations and those with low digital literacy. We also have experience with inclusive design audits (accessibility for users with visual, motor, or cognitive impairments), which aligns with your mention of disability-inclusive design — a dimension that will distinguish the proposal.

4.1 Impact Alignment

The proposal's impact narrative must convincingly address the call's expected outcomes. We see the following impact logic:

    Short-term (project lifetime): Demonstrable reductions in water consumption (target: 20–30%) and mineral fertiliser use (target: 25–40%) at pilot sites, enabled by precision monitoring, AI-DSS, and circular water/nutrient integration.
    Medium-term (3–5 years post-project): Scalable adoption across European farming systems through affordable, interoperable tools validated in diverse pedoclimatic zones, supported by training programmes and policy-ready recommendations.
    Long-term (systemic): Contribution to EU water and nutrient resilience targets under the European Green Deal, Farm to Fork Strategy, Zero Pollution Action Plan, and CAP eco-scheme frameworks.

The inclusion of reclaimed water as a resilience lever is particularly impactful in the current policy moment, given the revision of the Urban Wastewater Treatment Directive and the growing recognition that water reuse must be embedded in agricultural water security strategies — not treated as a niche option.
4.2 Strengthening the Proposal: Recommendations

Based on our experience with successful Horizon Europe proposals in Cluster 6, we offer the following recommendations for consortium development:

    Secure a strong SSH partner early — not as an afterthought. Evaluation Study Groups consistently penalise proposals where SSH is relegated to a minor task. A dedicated SSH partner (preferably with agricultural sociology or science-and-technology-studies expertise) should co-author the co-design and adoption WPs from the start.

    Ensure farmer organisations are formal partners, not merely "associated" — the multi-actor approach requires that farmers and their representatives are embedded in governance, not just consulted. At least 2–3 farmer cooperatives or producer organisations should be full beneficiaries.

    Include an environmental/biodiversity assessment dimension — the call explicitly references pollution and biodiversity impacts. A partner with ecological monitoring capabilities (e.g., pollinator surveys, soil biodiversity indicators, freshwater ecology) strengthens the environmental credibility.

    Plan the business model and exploitation strategy from Day 1 — Bondalti's industrial profile is a significant asset here. The proposal should articulate a credible commercialisation pathway for the DSS, monitoring systems, and reuse configurations, including licensing models, SaaS pricing, or integration into existing platform ecosystems.

    Address the organic farming dimension — your keyword list includes organic farming, which is wise given the call's alignment with the Farm to Fork 25% organic target. Ensure at least one demo site operates under organic certification to test how proposed tools perform under organic constraints (no synthetic fertilisers, different pest/nutrient management logic).
