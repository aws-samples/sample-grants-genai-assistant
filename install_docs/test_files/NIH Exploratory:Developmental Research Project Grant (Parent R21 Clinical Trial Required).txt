Project Title: AI-Guided Precision Modulation of the Gut-Brain Axis for Treatment-Resistant Major Depressive Disorder: An Exploratory Randomized Clinical Trial
Statement of the Problem

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) affects approximately 21 million adults in the United States annually, with roughly 30% of patients failing to achieve remission after two or more adequate antidepressant trials — a condition designated treatment-resistant depression (TRD). Current pharmacological interventions primarily target monoaminergic neurotransmission, yet accumulating evidence implicates the gut-brain axis (GBA) as a critical, underexploited mediator of mood regulation, neuroinflammation, and treatment response. Despite promising preclinical and observational human data linking gut microbiome composition to depressive symptomatology, no precision-guided, microbiome-directed intervention has been rigorously tested in a controlled clinical trial specifically targeting TRD patients stratified by their individual microbiome-immune-neural signatures.
Knowledge Gap and Opportunity

Three converging lines of evidence create an unprecedented opportunity:

    Microbiome-depression associations are reproducible but heterogeneous. Large-cohort studies (e.g., Flemish Gut Flora Project, N>1,000; Valles-Colomer et al., Nature Microbiology, 2019) consistently identify microbial taxa (e.g., Coprococcus, Dialister, Faecalibacterium) depleted in depression, yet effect sizes vary enormously across individuals — suggesting that population-level probiotic approaches are insufficient.

    Gut-derived metabolites directly modulate neuroinflammatory pathways. Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), tryptophan metabolites (kynurenine, indole derivatives), and secondary bile acids cross the blood-brain barrier or signal via the vagus nerve, influencing microglial activation, serotonin synthesis, and HPA axis reactivity — pathways centrally implicated in TRD pathophysiology.

    Machine learning can now predict individual microbiome-metabolite-clinical trajectories. Recent advances in multi-omic integration and interpretable AI models make it feasible, for the first time, to design individually tailored synbiotic formulations (targeted prebiotic + probiotic combinations) based on a patient's baseline microbiome composition, metabolomic profile, and inflammatory markers.

Yet these three streams have never been synthesized into a single interventional framework tested in a clinical trial.
Innovation and Risk-Reward Profile

This project is inherently exploratory and carries considerable risk: the causal directionality of GBA-depression associations remains debated, individual microbiome responses to synbiotic interventions are variable, and the AI-guided personalization framework is novel and unvalidated in psychiatry. However, if successful, this work would represent a paradigm shift — moving psychiatric treatment of TRD from symptom-based pharmacology to mechanism-based, biologically personalized gut-brain intervention, with implications extending to anxiety disorders, PTSD, and neurodegenerative conditions.
Specific Aims

Aim 1: Characterize the multi-omic gut-brain signatures that distinguish TRD from treatment-responsive MDD and healthy controls. Using a deeply phenotyped cross-sectional cohort (N=120; 40 TRD, 40 treatment-responsive MDD, 40 matched healthy controls), we will generate integrated microbiome (16S rRNA + shotgun metagenomics), metabolomic (targeted SCFAs, tryptophan/kynurenine pathway, bile acids), inflammatory (cytokine panel, CRP, IL-6, TNF-α), and neural (resting-state fMRI connectivity, EEG frontal alpha asymmetry) profiles. We will apply multi-modal machine learning (MOFA+, network-based integration) to identify TRD-specific GBA signatures and candidate mechanistic pathways.

Aim 2: Develop and computationally validate an AI-guided algorithm for personalized synbiotic formulation selection. Using Aim 1 data plus published microbiome-intervention response datasets, we will train and internally validate a recommender algorithm that predicts, for a given individual's baseline multi-omic profile, the optimal synbiotic formulation (from a predefined library of 6 evidence-based combinations) most likely to normalize their specific GBA dysregulation pattern. Algorithm performance will be benchmarked against random assignment and population-average assignment using cross-validated prediction accuracy for metabolomic shift toward healthy-control profiles.

Aim 3: Conduct a proof-of-concept, double-blind, randomized controlled trial (N=60 TRD patients) comparing AI-guided personalized synbiotic intervention versus active placebo over 12 weeks. Primary outcome: change in Montgomery-Åsberg Depression Rating Scale (MADRS) score at 12 weeks. Secondary outcomes: microbiome compositional shift, metabolomic normalization index, inflammatory marker reduction, resting-state functional connectivity changes (default mode network–salience network coupling), and tolerability/adverse events. Exploratory outcome: identification of baseline predictors of response to inform future, larger trials.

2.1 Significance

Burden and unmet need. TRD imposes catastrophic individual, societal, and economic costs — estimated at $44 billion annually in the US in direct and indirect costs. Existing escalation strategies (lithium augmentation, atypical antipsychotic add-on, ketamine/esketamine, ECT, TMS) carry significant side-effect burdens, are expensive, require specialized clinical settings, or have durability concerns. A safe, biologically personalized, orally administered intervention targeting the mechanistic substrate of TRD — rather than adding another monoaminergic or glutamatergic agent — would fundamentally alter the treatment landscape.

Advancing mechanistic understanding. Even if the clinical signal is modest (expected for an R21-scale trial), this project will generate the most deeply phenotyped, longitudinally sampled, multi-omic GBA dataset in TRD to date, with pre- and post-intervention samples enabling causal inference approaches (mediation analysis: does microbiome shift mediate mood improvement? Does metabolomic change precede or follow clinical response?). This dataset, which we will make publicly available through the NIMH Data Archive (NDA) and the Sequence Read Archive (SRA), will serve as a foundational resource for the field.

Alignment with NIH strategic priorities. This project directly addresses NIMH's Strategic Plan Objective 3 (developing new treatments based on understanding of mental illness mechanisms) and Objective 4 (strengthening the public health impact of NIMH-supported research through clinical trials). It also aligns with NCCIH's interest in the human microbiome and with ODP's Nutrition Research priorities. The proposed mechanistic clinical trial fits squarely within the R21 (clinical trial required) mechanism and meets the NIH definition of a clinical trial (prospective biomedical research assigning human participants to an intervention to evaluate effects on health-related biomedical outcomes).

Rigor and reproducibility. We address the call's emphasis on exploratory yet rigorous research through: pre-registration (ClinicalTrials.gov prior to enrollment), a priori power analysis (see Approach), CONSORT-compliant reporting, and FAIR-compliant data sharing. We employ validated clinical outcome measures (MADRS, administered by trained raters blind to allocation), standardized biological sample collection protocols (Earth Microbiome Project standards for stool; HUSERMET protocols for metabolomics), and reproducible bioinformatic pipelines (Snakemake workflows, containerized environments).
2.2 Innovation

This project is innovative in concept, approach, and potential application:

Conceptual innovation. No prior study has attempted to close the loop between diagnostic multi-omic profiling, AI-driven personalized intervention selection, and prospective clinical validation in a single TRD-focused protocol. Existing microbiome-depression trials use one-size-fits-all probiotic strains (e.g., Lactobacillus rhamnosus, Bifidobacterium longum) selected based on population-average associations. Our approach recognizes that the GBA is not a single target but an individualized ecosystem requiring personalized modulation — a conceptual leap analogous to the shift from empirical chemotherapy to biomarker-guided precision oncology.

Methodological innovation.

    Multi-omic integration for patient stratification: We pioneer the application of Multi-Omics Factor Analysis (MOFA+) combined with weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA) adapted for microbial-metabolomic networks to define GBA "endotypes" in TRD — a methodology not previously applied in psychiatric clinical trials.
    AI-guided synbiotic selection: Our recommender algorithm uses Bayesian optimization over a biologically constrained formulation space, incorporating prior knowledge of strain-substrate-metabolite relationships (from gnotobiotic mouse models and in vitro fermentation data) as informative priors — a hybrid human-knowledge/data-driven approach more appropriate for small-sample clinical settings than purely data-driven deep learning.
    Longitudinal dense sampling: Participants provide stool, blood, and clinical assessments at baseline, 2, 4, 8, and 12 weeks — enabling dynamic trajectory modeling (Gaussian process regression) of microbiome-metabolome-mood co-evolution, which no prior trial has captured at this resolution.

Technical innovation.

    Predefined synbiotic library: Rather than attempting fully bespoke formulations (infeasible at this stage), we have rationally designed a library of 6 synbiotic formulations, each targeting a distinct GBA dysregulation pattern identified in preliminary data (see Preliminary Data). This constrained personalization approach is clinically translatable and regulatorily tractable.
    Neuroimaging-microbiome integration: We include resting-state fMRI to test whether microbiome-directed intervention alters brain network connectivity patterns previously associated with TRD (hyperconnectivity within the default mode network, decoupling from salience and executive control networks) — providing a neural mechanistic endpoint alongside clinical and biological outcomes.

3.1 Overall Study Design

This project unfolds in two sequential phases across the 2-year R21 period:

Phase 1 (Months 1–10): Cross-sectional deep phenotyping and algorithm development (Aims 1 & 2) Phase 2 (Months 8–24): Proof-of-concept RCT (Aim 3) Note: Phase overlap in Months 8–10 allows algorithm finalization while RCT recruitment begins.
3.2 Aim 1: Multi-Omic GBA Signatures in TRD

Participants. N=120 adults (ages 25–65) recruited from the [Institution] Mood Disorders Clinic and surrounding community:

    TRD group (n=40): DSM-5 MDD diagnosis, failure of ≥2 adequate antidepressant trials (assessed via Massachusetts General Hospital Antidepressant Treatment Response Questionnaire), current MADRS ≥20.
    Treatment-responsive MDD group (n=40): DSM-5 MDD with documented remission (MADRS <10) on first or second antidepressant trial.
    Healthy controls (n=40): No lifetime psychiatric diagnosis (SCID-5 screened), matched for age, sex, BMI, and diet quality (Healthy Eating Index-2020).

Exclusion criteria: Antibiotic use within 3 months; probiotic/prebiotic supplementation within 1 month; inflammatory bowel disease; bariatric surgery; current substance use disorder; pregnancy; unstable medical conditions.

Biological sampling.

    Stool: Collected using OMNIgene•GUT kits (stabilized at room temperature); processed within 24 hours for 16S rRNA V4 amplicon sequencing (Illumina MiSeq; ~50,000 reads/sample) and shotgun metagenomic sequencing (Illumina NovaSeq; ~5 Gbp/sample).
    Blood: Fasting venipuncture for serum metabolomics (targeted LC-MS/MS panel: SCFAs [acetate, propionate, butyrate], tryptophan, kynurenine, kynurenic acid, quinolinic acid, indole-3-propionic acid, primary/secondary bile acids) and inflammatory markers (high-sensitivity CRP, IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-10, by Luminex multiplex).
    Neuroimaging: 10-minute resting-state fMRI (3T Siemens Prisma, multiband EPI, TR=800ms, 2mm isotropic) and T1-weighted structural scan. Preprocessing via fMRIPrep; connectivity analysis focused on DMN, salience network, and central executive network using the Schaefer 400-parcel atlas.

Analysis plan.

    Differential abundance testing (ALDEx2, ANCOM-BC2) for microbial taxa, with FDR correction.
    Pathway-level metagenomic analysis (HUMAnN 3.0) for functional capacity.
    Between-group metabolomic comparisons (limma with empirical Bayes moderation).
    Multi-omic integration via MOFA+ to identify latent factors capturing co-variation across microbiome, metabolome, inflammation, and neural connectivity.
    Network analysis (WGCNA-adapted for mixed data types) to identify hub features and candidate causal mediators.
    Deliverable: A set of 3–5 GBA "endotype" signatures characteristic of TRD, with candidate mechanistic pathways nominated for targeted intervention.

3.3 Aim 2: AI-Guided Personalized Synbiotic Recommender

Synbiotic library. Based on preliminary data and published evidence, we have pre-designed 6 formulations:
Formulation	Target Endotype	Probiotic Component	Prebiotic Component
SYN-1	Low butyrate/SCFA producers	F. prausnitzii + Roseburia intestinalis	Inulin + resistant starch
SYN-2	Tryptophan-kynurenine shunt	L. reuteri + B. infantis	Tryptophan-enriched FOS
SYN-3	Bile acid dysmetabolism	L. rhamnosus + C. scindens	UDCA + GOS
SYN-4	Pro-inflammatory dominance	B. longum + A. muciniphila	Polyphenol-rich prebiotic blend
SYN-5	Combined SCFA + kynurenine	SYN-1 + SYN-2 components	Combined prebiotics
SYN-6	Broad dysbiosis (low diversity)	Multi-strain consortium (8 spp.)	Diverse fiber blend

Algorithm development. We will train a Bayesian recommender model that takes as input a patient's baseline feature vector (microbiome composition at genus level, metabolomic panel, inflammatory markers) and outputs a probability distribution over the 6 formulations, representing predicted likelihood of achieving metabolomic normalization (shift toward healthy-control centroid in MOFA+ latent space). We use a Gaussian process surrogate model with informative priors derived from: (a) published in vitro fermentation studies linking specific substrates to metabolite production by specific taxa; (b) gnotobiotic mouse colonization data from our lab (see Preliminary Data); (c) the cross-sectional associations from Aim 1. Internal validation: leave-one-out cross-validation within Aim 1 data, benchmarked against random assignment and majority-class assignment.

Rigor consideration. We acknowledge that Aim 1 sample size (N=120) limits the complexity of models that can be robustly trained. Our approach mitigates this by: (a) constraining the output space to 6 discrete choices (not continuous formulation space); (b) incorporating strong biological priors rather than learning de novo; (c) using Bayesian uncertainty quantification to flag patients for whom no formulation is confidently recommended (these patients would be randomized among formulations in the RCT, enriching the training set for future iterations).
3.4 Preliminary Data

Our team has generated compelling preliminary data supporting feasibility:

    Pilot microbiome profiling in TRD (n=28 TRD, n=25 HC; PI's R01-funded cohort): We identified significant depletion of Coprococcus eutactus (LDA effect size=3.8, p<0.001), Faecalibacterium (LDA=3.2, p=0.004), and enrichment of Eggerthella lenta (LDA=3.5, p=0.002) in TRD, with corresponding reductions in fecal butyrate (p=0.008) and indole-3-propionic acid (p=0.02). Crucially, these differences were not uniformly present — supporting the endotype hypothesis.

    In vitro fermentation model: Using the SHIME® system inoculated with fecal samples from 10 TRD patients, we demonstrated that patient-specific prebiotic combinations (selected based on baseline microbiome composition) produced significantly greater SCFA increases than a generic prebiotic (p=0.01, paired t-test) — proof-of-concept for personalized synbiotic selection.

    Neuroimaging-microbiome correlation (pilot, n=20 MDD): We observed significant associations between fecal butyrate levels and DMN-salience network coupling strength (r=−0.52, p=0.018), suggesting that microbiome-derived metabolites may influence the specific neural circuit dysregulation characteristic of depression.

4.1 Aim 3: Proof-of-Concept Randomized Controlled Trial

Design. Phase II, double-blind, parallel-group RCT comparing:

    Arm A — AI-guided personalized synbiotic (n=30): Formulation selected by the Aim 2 algorithm based on each participant's baseline multi-omic profile.
    Arm B — Active placebo (n=30): Microcrystalline cellulose capsules matched in appearance, with a low-dose, non-targeted prebiotic (2g maltodextrin) to maintain blinding (participants expect to receive a supplement).

Note: We deliberately chose an active placebo over a non-targeted synbiotic comparator to isolate the effect of personalization in this initial proof-of-concept. A future, larger R01 trial would include a non-personalized synbiotic arm.

Participants. N=60 adults meeting TRD criteria (same as Aim 1 TRD group). Participants from Aim 1's TRD group who remain eligible may enroll (expected ~25), with additional recruitment to reach N=60. All participants continue their existing antidepressant regimen unchanged (stable dose ≥8 weeks prior to enrollment and throughout the trial). This pragmatic design tests whether personalized synbiotic augmentation improves outcomes beyond stable pharmacotherapy.

Randomization and blinding. Computer-generated block randomization (block size 4), stratified by sex and baseline MADRS severity (20–30 vs. >30). Allocation concealment via REDCap randomization module. Participants, clinical raters, and data analysts are blinded. Only the unblinded pharmacist preparing individualized supplement packs has allocation knowledge.

Intervention. 12-week daily oral synbiotic (2 capsules, morning, with food) or active placebo. Compliance monitored via pill count, daily app-based self-report, and capsule riboflavin biomarker in urine (spot checks at weeks 4 and 8).

4.2 Statistical Analysis Plan

Power analysis. For the primary outcome (MADRS change, baseline to week 12), assuming a between-group difference of 5 points (clinically meaningful; prior microbiome-depression meta-analysis reports pooled effect d=0.45), SD=8, α=0.05, two-tailed: N=52 provides 80% power (independent-samples t-test). Enrolling N=60 accommodates 15% attrition.

Primary analysis. Linear mixed-effects model with MADRS score as outcome, fixed effects for treatment group, time, and group×time interaction, random intercept per participant. Intention-to-treat population. Sensitivity analyses: per-protocol population; multiple imputation for missing data; adjustment for baseline MADRS, sex, and BMI.

Secondary/mechanistic analyses.

    Microbiome: Bray-Curtis dissimilarity and PERMANOVA for overall compositional shift; ALDEx2 for differential abundance at each timepoint.
    Metabolomic normalization index: Mahalanobis distance of each participant's metabolomic profile from the healthy-control centroid (from Aim 1), compared between arms over time.
    Mediation analysis (causal inference framework): Does microbiome compositional change at week 4 mediate MADRS improvement at week 12? Does metabolomic shift mediate the microbiome-mood relationship? (Structural equation modeling with bootstrapped confidence intervals.)
    Neuroimaging: Paired comparison of DMN-salience network connectivity change from baseline to week 12, between arms.

Exploratory: Algorithm performance evaluation — did the algorithm's confidence score predict clinical response? Were there endotype×formulation interactions?
4.3 Rigor, Reproducibility, and Transparency

    Pre-registration: Full protocol and statistical analysis plan registered on ClinicalTrials.gov prior to first enrollment.
    Data sharing: De-identified clinical, microbiome, metabolomic, and neuroimaging data deposited in NIMH Data Archive (NDA), SRA, and MetaboLights within 12 months of study completion, per NIH data sharing policy.
    Code sharing: All bioinformatic and statistical pipelines deposited on GitHub with DOI (Zenodo archive).
    Biological sex as a variable: All analyses will test for sex×treatment interactions, given known sex differences in GBA function and depression.
    Authentication of biological resources: Probiotic strains verified by whole-genome sequencing and viability assurance at time of encapsulation and at 6-month stability testing.

4.4 Potential Limitations and Mitigation
Limitation	Mitigation
Small sample size limits generalizability	R21 is explicitly proof-of-concept; we power for a meaningful effect size and generate rich mechanistic data to inform a future R01
Individual microbiome variability may overwhelm treatment signal	Personalization approach specifically addresses this; dense longitudinal sampling enables within-subject trajectory analysis
Placebo response in depression trials is high (~30%)	We use active placebo, clinician-rated primary outcome (less susceptible than self-report), and augmentation design (baseline pharmacotherapy controlled)
AI algorithm trained on limited data	Bayesian framework with biological priors; uncertainty quantification prevents overconfident recommendations
Participants' diets may confound microbiome changes	Dietary assessment (ASA24) at three timepoints; dietary counseling to maintain habitual diet; diet included as covariate

4.7 Significance of Exploratory Outcomes

Regardless of whether the RCT demonstrates a statistically significant clinical benefit — a high bar for N=60 — this project will deliver:

    The first multi-omic GBA endotype classification in TRD, publicly available.
    The first AI-guided personalized synbiotic recommender for a psychiatric indication, with open-source code.
    The first longitudinal, densely sampled microbiome-metabolome-neuroimaging dataset in a TRD intervention trial.
    Critical feasibility, effect-size, and variance estimates for powering a subsequent fully powered R01 multi-site trial.

This R21 lays the essential groundwork for a new therapeutic paradigm — one in which the gut ecosystem is not merely correlated with mental illness but becomes a precision-targeted treatment lever.
